In our lives

Things you can do right now

Turn on auto updates

Just update your stuff. Seriously.

We all dislike them - humans have an automatic averse reaction to change - but it holds true that updates are critical to safety, and companies want your applications and computer to be safe, as it keeps them from getting sued.

Use HTTPS Everywhere

Hyper Text Transfer Protocol is essential for browsers to communicate with web-servers. You can read more about HTTP here. As you could probably imagine, this communication between computers isn’t always secure. The creators of HTTP knew this, so that’s why they created HTTPS, which is just a form of HTTP where all that communication in encrypted in SSL. If you are using chrome, there's an easy extension you can install to do this for you: HTTPS everywhere.

Check if your accounts have been hacked

On the trusted website haveibeenpwned.com (link), you can input your usernames and emails to check if they’ve been compromised via data breaches. They also have an expansive list of all the major data breaches of the internet age here.

Clear your cache/use incognito

As previously explained, websites can track you using cookies they place on your browser. If you don’t want this to happen, clear your cache whenever you use your browser. Alternatively, you can just use incognito whenever you don’t want to be tracked.

Things that will take 5 minutes

Change your privacy settings

Many popular media sites allow your info to be publicly available and sold to advertising companies. (read summaries of privacy policies here). Luckily, many of these websites allow you to shut off their ability to track you and use your data, even if that ability is activated by default. You should spend 5 minutes turning off basic facebook and google privacy settings, and if you want you can send more time with your more obscure web sites.

Use an alphanumeric lock on your phone

Switch away from your 4-number or short gestured phone lock - those are easy to figure out. Instead, try using a longer, alphanumeric password. This works especially well on fingerprint-id phones, as whenever your hands are free you can just use your fingerprints instead of typing a longer password in.

Use a different browser/search engine

Sadly, websites are not your only privacy problem when online. The very browser you use also matters, perhaps more than what websites you visit. Every major browser tracks you to varying degrees, and all of them have your information on file. Don’t freak out, however, as the worst these companies will do with your info is sell it to add companies. It isn't your most sensitive info, either - mostly just anonymous basics.

Things that will take half an hour

Password Selection

Password selection is both critical to your safety and very poorly misunderstood among most consumers. Passwords need to be both long and complicated. As Edward Snowden said in this interview (probably NSFW), a better term to describe what they should be is pass-phrase. Try to think of a common phrase or quote from someone you like, and use the entire quote as your password. Think: YourfreetrialatAlbertEinsteinquotes.comhasexpired Rather than e=mc123

Another way of create passwords is to use a password scheme and use different ones for each account. For example, your scheme could be two words and two numbers. The first word would be a food and the second word would be an activity. Then, the password for your first account could be “pineappleskiing42” and the password for a different account could be “teriyakitennis99”, and so forth.

Another potential solution to the unsecure password problem is to use a password manager.

You’ve probably heard of these - they’re cross-platform applications that basically serve as a huge storage vault for all your passwords. It takes one memorized password to get into the manager, but once you’re there you can just copy paste your passwords for whatever account you’re trying to get into. Because of this, you can use long, complicated passwords for all your accounts without having to remember each one.

But are they safe?

Short answer - kinda.

Most password managers use AES 256 encryption system to keep your passwords safe. AES is a fantastic system, and completely safe. You can read more about it here. No-one will be taking your passwords by brute force attacks, that’s for sure. The applications themselves, however, have been known to contain several security issues.

Despite this, almost all online cybersecurity experts recommend using password managers. They are simply a much safer bet than using passwords on your own. Just make sure the password you use to get into your manager is a good one.

So what do you use? Well, choosing which manager to use is not choosing a ‘best’ product, but instead choosing between usability and cost. Just like an apple product, the most streamlined experiences are always the most expensive - and also non customizable.

There is a free management system that works perfectly well. It’s called KeePass (or KeePassX on mac), but it requires a lot of setup to start working. Here's a guide for Windows, Mac , and Linux.

Set up 2 factor identification

When possible 2 factor identification is one of the safest possible ways to protect your accounts. Online hackers can’t physically steal your phone, and those who would steal your phone most likely don’t have the capabilities to hack your accounts. It takes a little while to get used to, and isn’t available everywhere, but for the sites that have it, it can be an invaluable tool to use for your protection.

Run First aid

If you're on a mac: go to applications, and open disc utility. Click on your named internal folder, and then click first aid in the middle of the top of the application. Click ok a few times, and then go read a book for thirty minutes. This cleans your computer of bugs and inconsistencies, and you may notice a slight speed boost after using it. Do this every month or so - it can be very helpful and important for security.

If you're on windows, we recommend using any anti-virus software to scan your computer.

Learning safe habits:

Yes, of course this is on the list. None of these privacy methods matter if you don’t put them into practice. While changing one password to your favorite Whitman poem may make you feel good, none of it matters if you don’t change most, if not all, of your passwords. If you’re going to clear your cache to remove cookies, you better do it more then just once. If you actually want to be safe, then you need to create a weekly or monthly process of these habits, not just whenever you want to feel safe.

But what about VPNs?

While VPNs have many benefits, they often give a false sense of security. A good VPN will protect you from the average person from snooping around in your web traffic, but most of the time your data is still being logged. This becomes a gray area, where we recommend you trust a VPN with your own discretion. However, VPNs do provide some significant benefits - it eliminates using unsecure wifi networks and potential viruses.

A VPN is basically an application that re-routes your ip, data requests, and information through another encrypted server somewhere else in the world. This means that your computer can not be hacked by someone on the same wifi network, and to hackers you will be seen using the computer in an entirely different area, protecting your data and location.

For people who want to be extra careful, we would recommend running your own VPN.
